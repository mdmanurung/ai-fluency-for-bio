Overview

Course description

AI tools — especially large language models (LLMs) and AI coding assistants — are rapidly entering the biology research workflow. Used well, they can accelerate analysis, surface relevant literature, and help draft code and documentation. Used carelessly, they introduce subtle errors, fabricated citations, and workflow brittleness.

This course teaches AI fluency foundations for biology: the framework, vocabulary, and habits to collaborate with AI tools productively and responsibly. It is a starting point. Real fluency comes from sustained practice with feedback over months — the course is designed to make that practice deliberate.

Who this course is for

  • Graduate students, postdocs, PIs, and staff scientists in life sciences.
  • Researchers who already use, or want to use, tools like Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Cursor, or agentic coding assistants.
  • No prior AI/ML background required. Working comfort with R or Python at a scripting level is helpful for the hands-on weeks.

Scope and bias

The conceptual content (4 D’s, LLM literacy, ethics) is subfield-agnostic. The hands-on project is a complete single-cell RNA-seq pipeline on the 10x PBMC 3k dataset — chosen because the tooling is mature, the dataset is small enough to run in a free-tier Colab, and the workflow exercises every step of the AI-fluency framework (delegating boilerplate, describing dataset structure, discerning thresholds, owning interpretation).

Students from other subfields (structural, microbial, ecological, neural, developmental) are welcome and encouraged to bring their own datasets to the capstone. The 4 D’s transfer; the scRNA-seq concrete project is the scaffold, not the limit.

Learning outcomes

By the end of this course, participants will be able to:

# Outcome Bloom level Evidenced by
1 Apply the 4 D’s framework to classify a research task and choose a delegation pattern. Apply Week 1 reflection
2 Explain at a working level how LLMs are trained, generate text, and fail. Understand Week 2 exercise
3 Construct structured prompts (system, few-shot, tool-use) for bio research tasks. Apply Week 2 exercise
4 Use an AI coding assistant to scaffold and debug an scRNA-seq analysis. Apply Week 3 mini-project
5 Evaluate AI-generated analyses, citations, and protocols for correctness, provenance, and methodological soundness. Evaluate Week 3 mini-project + week 4 capstone
6 Produce a reproducible, AI-assisted research deliverable with a complete disclosure statement. Create Week 4 capstone
7 Critique the ethical, epistemic, and reproducibility implications of a given AI use in research. Evaluate Throughout, formalized in capstone reflection

Course structure

The course is organised into three conceptual tracks, a 5-module scRNA-seq pipeline, and four weekly meetings that braid them together.

  • The AI fluency track introduces the 4 D’s framework.
  • The AI literacy track covers the technical and conceptual background.
  • The Hands-on bioinformatics track is the AI-fluency lens applied to four research tasks (code, data, literature, protocols).
  • The scRNA-seq pipeline is the concrete project: 5 modules from FASTQ to annotated UMAP on 10x PBMC 3k. Modules 1–2 are pre-week-3 reading; Module 3 anchors week 3 in-class; Module 4 is week 3 after-class; Module 5 is one valid path for the week 4 capstone.
  • The weekly meetings combine readings, live demos, and workshops from the three tracks and the modules.

See the Syllabus for policies, assessment, the AI-use rubric, and the reading list. See the Schedule for week-by-week topics.
